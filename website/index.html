What Is A DPP

What is a Digital Product Passport?

In simple words, a DPP is a digital identity card for a product. It links a physical product to trusted digital information through a QR code or another data carrier, so people can understand what the product is made of, how it should be used, repaired, handled, and eventually recovered.

One place for product truth

Instead of chasing PDFs, spreadsheets, declarations, and supplier emails, the passport gathers core product data in one structured record.

Useful for more than compliance

It supports customers, service teams, recyclers, and internal business users, not just regulators.

Built for the full lifecycle

The passport can stay relevant from raw material sourcing all the way to repair, resale, recycling, and feedstock recovery.

Why It Became Mandatory

Why DPP was made mandatory

The EU is using Digital Product Passports because circularity goals cannot be managed well with fragmented product data. Policymakers want more reliable information to follow products through supply chains, use, repair, and end-of-life.

To make sustainability claims verifiable

ESPR (EU) 2024/1781

ESPR created the framework for product passports so sustainability, durability, repairability, and circularity data can be shared in a structured and machine-readable way instead of being hidden in disconnected documents.

To improve battery transparency first

Battery Regulation (EU) 2023/1542

The battery rules make the first concrete DPP mandate operational. They connect market access to product-level data on carbon footprint, recycled content, due diligence, and end-of-life handling.

To support repair, reuse, and recycling

Circular economy objective

The passport helps products stay understandable after sale, making it easier for service teams, refurbishers, and recyclers to know what is inside the product and what should happen next.

To reduce blind spots in value chains

REACH, RoHS, waste, due diligence data

Many product obligations depend on data from suppliers, substances, waste flows, and environmental studies. DPPs give companies a practical way to maintain that evidence over time instead of recreating it under deadline pressure.

Key Regulations Driving DPP

The regulation picture, kept practical

The core DPP framework is now real law, but not every product track is at the same maturity level. Some are already fixed, some are in phased application, and some still depend on secondary acts or legislative finalisation.

Framework

ESPR

Regulation (EU) 2024/1781 entered into force on 18 July 2024 and created the main Digital Product Passport framework for future product-specific rules under the ecodesign system.

First live DPP mandate

EU Battery Regulation

Regulation (EU) 2023/1542 is the first product law with a concrete DPP rollout. Battery passport obligations for relevant industrial and EV batteries apply from 18 February 2027.

Outside ESPR

Construction Products Regulation

Regulation (EU) 2024/3110 introduces a digital product passport route for construction products through its own legal track, which makes this an important adjacent passport market beyond ESPR.

Outside ESPR

Detergents recast

The revised detergents framework adds digital product passport and digital labelling logic for detergent products, making it another product-specific passport stream companies should watch closely.

Legislative track

Toy safety

The proposed Toy Safety Regulation includes a digital product passport approach. It is a serious signal for future passport expansion, but it should be described as a legislative track until final adoption is complete.

Connected data rules

REACH, RoHS, waste, and due diligence

These rules do not always create the passport themselves, but they often define the substance, lifecycle, and evidence data that the passport must eventually carry or support.

We checked this section against official EU sources before rewriting it. The important nuance is that batteries and the new construction products route are concrete legal tracks, while toys and several future sectors still depend on ongoing legislative or delegated-act work.
What A DPP Must Contain

What a DPP must contain

Exact fields differ by product law, but most passport programmes are built from the same recurring data blocks: identity, materials, environmental evidence, compliance proof, lifecycle instructions, and governed updates over time.

🪪

Product identity

Unique identifier, model or batch information, manufacturer details, and the data carrier that links the physical product to its passport.

⚗️

Material and substance data

Composition, origin declarations, recycled content, and where relevant substances of concern or restricted substances must be disclosed.

🌿

Environmental evidence

Carbon footprint, durability, circularity, repairability, and other sustainability indicators required by the applicable sector rule.

🔧

Use, repair, and end-of-life guidance

Instructions that help users, service teams, and recyclers handle the product correctly through use, maintenance, dismantling, and recovery.

📋

Compliance and documentary support

Declarations, test evidence, certifications, due diligence records, and links to supporting documents that back up the passport statements.

🔒

Governance and history

Controlled updates, version history, access logic, and audit trails so the passport remains trustworthy over the life of the product.
