Legislation in Force

The Key Regulations Driving DPP

These are not proposals or guidelines — they are enacted laws with binding deadlines and enforceable penalties.

📜

Ecodesign for Sustainable Products Regulation (ESPR)

Regulation (EU) 2024/1781 · In Force July 2024

ESPR replaces the Ecodesign Directive and establishes Digital Product Passports as mandatory for most product categories sold in the EU. It covers energy-related products, textiles, electronics, furniture, construction materials, chemicals, and more. Every DPP must be accessible via a unique data carrier (QR code or RFID) and contain structured sustainability data as specified in product-specific delegated acts.

🔋

EU Battery Regulation

Regulation (EU) 2023/1542 · DPP Mandatory Feb 2027

The first regulation to mandate Digital Product Passports. From February 18, 2027, all industrial batteries and EV batteries sold in the EU must carry a compliant DPP containing carbon footprint declarations, supply chain due diligence data, recycled content percentages, and end-of-life information. Carbon footprint declarations became enforceable in February 2025 for large stationary batteries.

🏢

Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive (CSDDD)

Directive (EU) 2024/1760 · Phased from 2027

CSDDD requires large companies to identify, prevent, and address adverse human rights and environmental impacts across their value chains. In practice, that means product-level evidence, controlled provenance records, and auditable passport data that can stand up to regulator and customer scrutiny.

⚗️

REACH Regulation & Substances of Concern

Regulation (EC) 1907/2006 · Ongoing + DPP Integration 2027

ESPR mandates that DPPs disclose all Substances of Very High Concern (SVHCs) present in products above 0.1% by weight — directly integrating REACH obligations into the passport data schema. This affects virtually every manufactured product and requires reliable material-level evidence inside the passport record.

🧩

Additional Product Rules Requiring Digital Passports

Toys · Construction Products · Detergents · Sector-specific acts

The DPP conversation is bigger than ESPR. Toys, construction products, detergents, and other regulated categories are moving toward structured digital passport requirements or passport-like product data obligations. A platform approach lets you reuse the same governance, viewer, audit trail, and analytics capabilities across each category instead of building one-off solutions.

6 Reasons You Cannot Ignore This

1

Market Access Blocked Without DPP

Products without valid DPPs will be prohibited from EU market placement. Customs authorities and market surveillance bodies will verify DPP validity at the border and point of sale — no exceptions for large or established brands.

2

Greenwashing is Now a Legal Liability

The EU Green Claims Directive criminalises unsubstantiated sustainability claims. Every green claim on packaging must be backed by verifiable DPP data — making the passport a legal shield, not just a compliance checkbox.

3

Data Aggregation Takes 18–36 Months

Collecting verified, audit-ready product evidence across materials, manufacturing, carbon, repair, and circularity workflows is not a quick project. Companies that wait until 12 months before deadline face impossible timelines and costly rushed implementations.

4

Financial Penalties Are Significant

Member states must impose effective, proportionate, and dissuasive penalties for non-compliance. Early indications from national regulators suggest penalties calculated as a percentage of annual EU turnover.

5

Investors and Procurement Demand It

CSRD-mandated sustainability reports require product-level environmental data. B2B procurement teams at major brands now include DPP readiness in supplier qualification criteria — making it a commercial prerequisite, not just regulatory.

6

Early Adopters Gain Competitive Advantage

Companies with DPPs can credibly market product sustainability, qualify for ecodesign incentives, streamline EPR reporting, and differentiate in procurement processes — turning compliance cost into commercial opportunity.

Data Requirements

What a DPP Must Contain

A Digital Product Passport is not a simple label — it is a structured, machine-readable data record covering the full product lifecycle. Here are the key data categories mandated under ESPR and Battery Regulation.

🌍

Material Origin & Composition

Country of origin, mining certifications, material composition percentages, and recycled content fractions. Required for REACH SVHC disclosure and Battery Reg recycled content mandates.

🌿

Carbon Footprint

Product-level carbon footprint declaration (kg CO₂e) across all lifecycle stages — raw materials, manufacturing, distribution, use, and end-of-life — following ISO 14067 or PEF methodology.

♻️

Recyclability & Circularity

Recyclability index, disassembly instructions, recycled content thresholds (e.g. 16% recycled cobalt by 2031 under Battery Reg), and end-of-life routing information for recyclers.

⚗️

Substances of Concern

All REACH SVHCs present above 0.1% threshold, RoHS restricted substances, and PFAS disclosures. Linked to unique substance identifiers (CAS numbers) for machine-readability.

🔧

Repairability & Durability

Spare parts availability, repair documentation, software update commitments (for electronic products), and durability ratings under ESPR minimum performance requirements.

🏭

Compliance Provenance & Circularity Evidence

Material provenance, sourcing certifications, recycled content evidence, repair records, and recovery outcomes needed for CSDDD, Battery Regulation, and product-category passport obligations.

Risk of Non-Compliance

What Happens If You Don't Comply

ESPR requires EU member states to establish national penalties for non-compliance. The regulation specifies they must be effective, proportionate, and dissuasive — and must account for the severity, duration, and extent of the infringement.

⚠️ Potential Consequences

Market Ban
Products without valid DPPs banned from EU market placement and sales
% Turnover
Penalties linked to annual EU-market turnover for continued infringement
Recall Orders
National authorities can order recall of non-compliant products already on market
Public Disclosure
Non-compliant economic operators publicly named in EU enforcement registers
