Regulation Timeline

Direct DPP mandates on the right, indirect but important rules on the left.

This timeline is designed to be more useful than a generic chronology. It separates the regulations that directly create passport obligations from the regulations and acts that shape the data, evidence, and compliance work behind the passport.

Dates below are described conservatively. Where a rule still depends on delegated acts, implementing acts, or legislative finalisation, we say so rather than pretending the final mandate is already fixed.

Indirect layer

REACH, RoHS, WEEE, and waste data remain core inputs

Even before sector passports go live, companies already need reliable material, substance, waste, and recovery data. These obligations often become the evidence layer that future passports must expose or support.

Ongoing baseline for passport data quality

2024
Direct framework

ESPR enters into force

Regulation (EU) 2024/1781 entered into force on 18 July 2024 and established the main legal framework for Digital Product Passports under the ecodesign system.

Framework now active
Indirect layer

Battery carbon footprint obligations begin phasing in

Under Regulation (EU) 2023/1542, carbon footprint declaration dates are staged and linked to secondary acts. Those requirements shape what battery passport programmes must be ready to ingest and evidence.

Data burden grows before the passport deadline itself
2025
Direct DPP tracks

Construction route opens and ESPR priorities become clearer

Regulation (EU) 2024/3110, the new Construction Products Regulation, entered into force in January 2025 and creates its own digital product passport route. On 16 April 2025, the Commission also adopted the first ESPR and Energy Labelling Working Plan, putting product groups such as textiles, furniture, tyres, iron and steel, and aluminium into focus.

Direct expansion beyond a single passport category
Indirect layer

Secondary acts and adjacent evidence rules keep moving

2026 is the kind of year where many companies feel the hidden workload: product-specific detail, unsold-consumer-product acts under ESPR, chemical restrictions, CE-linked evidence, and waste obligations all affect what teams must prepare.

This is usually where governance maturity becomes the bottleneck
2026
Direct DPP tracks

Construction application deepens; detergents and toys stay on watch

The construction passport route moves further into application. At the same time, detergents and toy safety remain important product-specific digital-data tracks, but they should still be described carefully where final legal steps are pending.

Strong adjacent passport market, mixed legal maturity
Indirect layer

Carbon classes, due diligence, and supporting evidence intensify

By the time battery passports arrive, the pressure is not only on the passport shell itself. Supporting carbon, due-diligence, material, and lifecycle data also has to hold up under scrutiny.

The passport becomes only as strong as the evidence behind it

2027
Direct hard deadline

Battery passports become mandatory

From 18 February 2027, each LMT battery, each industrial battery above 2 kWh, and each electric vehicle battery placed on the market or put into service must have a battery passport under Article 77 of Regulation (EU) 2023/1542.

First major hard DPP milestone
Indirect layer

Thresholds, classes, and environmental evidence keep tightening

After 2027, product carbon thresholds, performance classes, waste obligations, chemical updates, and other supporting requirements keep evolving. DPP work does not stop once the first passport goes live.

Governance shifts from launch project to ongoing capability

Direct expansion

More sector passports are expected to follow

The Commission’s ESPR prioritisation and the spread of sector-specific digital-product rules point toward broader DPP coverage over time. Textiles, furniture, industrial materials, and additional product-specific legal tracks remain key areas to watch.

Expansion wave rather than one single deadline

We checked this timeline against official EU materials before rewriting it. The biggest correction from the old version is that some sectors should be treated as priority pipelines or legislative tracks, not as already-final mandatory dates.
